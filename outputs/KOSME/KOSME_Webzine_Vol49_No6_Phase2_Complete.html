1. Bending moment measurement using strain gauges

Measurement of bending moments in ship propulsion shafting systems is a key aspect of assessing bearing reactions and shaft alignment. A propulsion shaft experiences various dynamic loads during operation, including propeller thrust, bearing reactions, and hull deflection effects, which can induce significant bending stresses. Excessive bending moments may lead to shaft misalignment, increased bearing loads, and fatigue damage, adversely affecting the overall efficiency and safety of the propulsion system.

In the previous work titled "Strain Gauge Measurements in Marine Applications" (KOSME Webzine, Vol. 49, No. 5), the strain gauge method was introduced as a high-precision technique applicable to various marine engineering fields. This method offers an effective and practical approach for measuring bending moments under actual operating conditions.

Strain gauges detect minute surface deformations and convert them into corresponding electrical signals, enabling direct evaluation of bending behavior. Strain gauges are typically arranged in a Wheatstone bridge circuit. The setup can be configured as a full-bridge, half-bridge, or quarter-bridge, depending on the specific measurement requirements.

Among the available configurations, a full-bridge setup is generally recommended for bending moment measurement compared to half- or quarter-bridge arrangements. By utilizing four active strain gauges, this configuration effectively compensates for thermal strains induced by temperature variations and axial strains generated by thrust forces.

2.2. Equivalent amplitude calculation using frequency domain

The above method using zero-to-peak value is very common and convenient to obtain the bending moment during static conditions such as slow turning. However, its accuracy is less in case of combined vibration as in a dynamic test.

In a ship propulsion system, the bending moment of the propeller shaft is influenced by many factors such as hull deformation, engine output, sea water wake field and propeller eccentric force. The local bending moment measured by the strain gauge is the sum of many sine waves.

The nominal bending moment amplitude during one revolution can be determined from the amplitude of the first harmonic component, while the corresponding bending angle is obtained from the phase of the same harmonic. To extract these parameters, frequency-domain analysis is required, with the Fourier transform serving as the fundamental mathematical tool.

2.3. True dynamic bending moment measurement

Direct readings from strain gauges are expressed in a rotating coordinate system attached to the shaft surface. To accurately assess the dynamic forces acting in both the vertical and horizontal directions at every rotational angle, it is essential to determine the true dynamic bending moment rather than its equivalent amplitude.

3. Bearing load identification

Bearings are critical components of marine propulsion systems, designed to sustain significant mechanical loads while maintaining alignment and smooth rotational motion. Under realistic operating conditions, propulsion shaft bearings are subjected to dynamic forces originating from propeller forces, shaft misalignment, and hull deformation.

Excessive bearing loads can result in elevated oil film temperatures, accelerated wear, and potential damage to bearing surfaces. Conversely, insufficient loading may lead to oil whirl and oil whip phenomena due to hydrodynamic instability. Therefore, precise identification of bearing reaction forces is essential for evaluating bearing performance, ensuring structural safety, and optimizing shaft alignment.

To achieve accurate load identification for a journal bearing, three strain gauge bridges are installed along the shaft at positions A, B, and C, located before and after the bearing. These bridges measure the vertical bending moments at their respective positions, denoted as M1, M2, and M3.

These findings are fundamental for assessing bearing alignment, evaluating load-carrying capacity, and verifying the safety margins of the propulsion line under different operating conditions.
